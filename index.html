Welcome Address

With the advent of high-throughput sequencing technologies, genomic sequencing has become a
common technique for determining genetic functions associated with evolution and diseases. This has
reduced the distance and time between bench and bedside, even as genome sequencing on dongles and
smartphones becomes commonplace! According to a July 2015 article in Washington Post, the wealth of
genomic data generated daily is doubling every seven months, so within the next decade, genomics may
generate somewhere between 2 and 40 exabytes a year. In context, the volume of genomic information
being created may eventually overtake internet sites like YouTube. Therefore, bioinformatic analysis of
high throughput genomic data is an emerging field with excellent career prospects. While opportunities for
bioinformatics and data science courses abound in richer settings, resources for genomic data analysis
are few and far between in most parts of Africa, including Nigeria. Our vision at the Genomic Research in
Biomedicine Laboratory, Nigerian Institute of Medical Research is to provide human and infrastructural
resources for data science and bioinformatics by training indigenous scientists and research scholars.
Given this background, we welcome you to the first edition of our Data Science in Biomedicine course
titled “Introduction to Bioinformatics and Genetic Data Analysis.” We wish you a remarkable learning
experience.

Kolapo Oyebola (PhD)
Coordinator

Dr. Oyebola has acquired training in genetics and analysis of next-gen sequence data in Africa, the UK and the US. He was the overall best PhD graduate in his graduating set at the University of Lagos, Nigeria. As part of his doctoral research, Dr Oyebola trained at the Medical Research Council at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine (MRCG-LSHTM) on genomics and bioinformatics techniques. He carried out microsatellite genotyping and genome-wide sequencing of malaria to understand patterns of variation and signatures of selection in south-western Nigeria. He was part of a global network of geneticists (MalariaGEN) involved in world-wide genome sequencing of malaria. Upon completion of his PhD, he won a Wellcome Trust-DELTAS Postdoctoral Fellowship. In addition, he received training from the H3Africa Bioinformatics Network on Genotyping Chip Data Analysis and genome-wide association studies of human populations. He also trained on Molecular SNP Barcoding at the Harvard School of Public Health and the Broad Institute, USA. He was an invited speaker at a 2016 TEDx meeting in Nigeria. Dr Oyebola is an Affiliate Fellow of the African Academy of Sciences. He is also a recipient of two competitive research grants from the European Union as well as Africa Research Excellence Fund. He is a research scientist at the NHLBI/NIH investigating mutations associated with certain cancer types in African-Americans living with sickle cell disease under a scheme jointly sponsored by the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation and the NIH. He is currently an NIH Emerging Global Leader (K43) grantee and the Head of the Genomic Research in Biomedicine Laboratory at the Nigerian Institute of Medical Research.
